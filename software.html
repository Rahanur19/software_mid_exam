📚 Software Engineering Fundamentals

Core concepts, definitions, and characteristics of software.

Definitions & Roles

  • 1. What is Software?
    Computer programs, data structures, and documentation that allow computers to perform tasks.
  • 2. Dual Role of Software:
    ▪️ As a Product: Delivers computing potential (functionality) to users.
    ▪️ As a Vehicle: Acts as the foundation/engine to deliver other products (e.g., OS, networks).
  • 3. Role of a SE:
    To apply systematic, disciplined, quantifiable approaches to the development, operation, and maintenance of software.
  • 4. Key Objectives:
    High quality, on time, within budget, maintainable, meets user needs.
  • 5. Sys Eng vs. Soft Eng:
    System Eng: Broad. Covers ALL aspects (Hardware, Software, People, Processes).
    Software Eng: Specific. Focuses only on building the software components.

Characteristics of Software

  • 6. Key Characteristics: Developed/engineered, doesn't wear out, custom-built.
  • 7. Engineered, not manufactured:
    Software is a logical entity. Costs lie in design & engineering, not in stamping out mass physical copies. Quality is built in during design.
  • 8. Doesn't "wear out":
    Unlike hardware (which breaks via friction/heat - "bathtub curve"), software is immune to physical degradation. The failure rate ideally drops to a flat line.
  • 9/10. Entropy & "Wearing Out":
    Bit Rot / Entropy: Software deteriorates logically. As changes/patches are made over time, complexity increases, architecture degrades, and bugs multiply. Environmental changes (new OS) also cause failures.
  • 11. Custom Built:
    Despite trends in reusing components (libraries/APIs), core business logic usually requires custom tailoring to specific client needs.

Evolution, Types & Ethics

12. Good SW Attributes:
  • Maintainability: Can evolve, easy to update.
  • Dependability: Secure, reliable, safe.
  • Efficiency: Doesn't waste memory/CPU.
  • Acceptability: Understandable, usable by target audience.
13/14. Software Crisis & Evolution:

Crisis: Difficulty writing defect-free, on-time software.
Causes: Rising complexity, poor management, changing reqs, lack of training.
Evolution: The continuous process of software adapting to environment changes.

15/16. Types of Software:

System: OS, Compilers.
Real-Time: Weather monitors, Air-traffic.
Application: (Word, Games).
App subtypes: Web, Mobile, Embedded, AI, Data.

17. Ethics:

Act in public interest, client best interest, maintain independence/integrity, fair to colleagues.

📊 Software Project Management (SPM)

Planning, scheduling, resource allocation, and risk management.

SPM Fundamentals

  • 1. What is SPM?
    The art and science of planning and leading software projects to successful completion.
  • 2. Need of SPM:
    Necessary to control cost, adhere to schedules, manage quality, and handle project complexity.
  • 4. PM Responsibilities:
    • Planning: Defining scope, timeline, budget.
    • Staffing: Assembling & leading the team.
    • Monitoring: Tracking progress vs plan.
  • 5. Key Activities:
    Project Estimation (Size/Cost), Scheduling (Gantt charts), Risk Management, Configuration Management.

3. The Iron Triangle

Changing one constraint affects the others (Quality is centralized).

⚠️ Risk Management Details

RM: The process of identifying, analyzing, and mitigating threats to a project's success. Essential to prevent failure.

T1: Risk Types & Definitions
Name Type Description
Tech Stack Technical Using untested/new technologies.
Budget Cuts Business Loss of funding mid-project.
Turnover Project Key team members leaving.
T2: 4 Stages of Risk Mgmt
# Stage Action
1 Identify Brainstorm potential risks (What can go wrong?).
2 Analyze Assess probability and impact severity.
3 Plan Develop mitigation strategies (How to prevent?).
4 Monitor Continuously track indicators during project.
T3: Modern Common Risks
Risk Factor Modern Context
Scope Creep Constant feature additions requested by clients.
Tech Debt Rushing code to meet Agile sprints causing bugs later.
Cyber Threat Vulnerabilities in 3rd party open-source libraries.
T4: Risk Response Strategies
Strategy Description
Avoid Change plan to eliminate risk (e.g., don't use new tech).
Mitigate Reduce probability/impact (e.g., train staff).
Transfer Shift burden to 3rd party (e.g., buy insurance, outsource).
Accept Acknowledge risk and budget for it (contingency fund).
T5: Risk Types & Potential Indicators
Risk Type Early Warning Indicator (Metric)
Schedule Risk Missed milestones, low sprint velocity.
Quality Risk High defect density in testing, failed builds.
Personnel Risk High absenteeism, missed daily standups.
